Machine Learning: A Short Primer

Machine learning is a branch of artificial intelligence that studies algorithms
which improve automatically through experience and by the use of data. The term
was coined by Arthur Samuel in 1959, while he was working at IBM on a program
that learned to play checkers.

Supervised learning is the most common paradigm. A model is trained on a labeled
dataset, where each example is paired with a target output. The goal is to learn a
mapping from inputs to outputs that generalizes to unseen data. Typical tasks
include classification and regression.

Unsupervised learning, by contrast, works with unlabeled data. The algorithm tries
to discover hidden structure, for example by grouping similar examples together.
Clustering and dimensionality reduction are classic unsupervised techniques.

Reinforcement learning is a third paradigm in which an agent interacts with an
environment and learns to maximize a cumulative reward signal. It underpins many
recent advances in game playing and robotics.

The transformer architecture was introduced in 2017 in the paper "Attention Is All
You Need". Transformers rely on a mechanism called self-attention, which allows the
model to weigh the importance of different parts of the input sequence. This design
became the foundation for large language models such as BERT and GPT.

Overfitting occurs when a model captures noise in the training data instead of the
underlying signal, and therefore performs poorly on new data. Common remedies
include regularization, early stopping, and gathering more training data.
